Visualizing Pittsburgh's Bus Bunching

They say bad news comes in threes. Sometimes, too, so do Pittsburgh's buses. In twos and threes, Pittsburgh's bunched buses bear the bad news of crowding (the front bus is often overfull, with subsequent buses nearly empty), waiting (bunching creates long waits between bus arrivals), and underutilization (crowding and waiting reduce public transit ridership). Bunching bedevils public transit planners not just in Pittsburgh but in cities the world over.

Does bunching follow predictable patterns? This project presents the geotemporal occurrence of bus bunching in Pittsburgh for selected routes in March 2016. The intent is to foster better understanding of where and when bunching occurs as a foundation for approaching the questions of "why does bunching occur" and "how do we make this better?"

The chart below shows bunching in time and space for Pittsburgh PAAC routes 61, 71, P1 and G2. The data is derived from vehicle location information collected for the month of March 1 - 27, 2016 (data collection details). Observations are defined as "bunched" if observed to be operating within 250m of another bus servicing the same line in the same direction.

The chart shows the frequency of bunching occurrences for buses travelling along their service routes. The data may be viewed based on the departure time of the bus's run (trip start), or based on the bus's location when the observation was captured (actual time).

White vertical lines indicate that no trips departed during the time window, corresponding with published schedules. The obvious red vertical lines indicate the recurrence of bunching for routes at certain departure times, and that bunching tends to persist throughout the duration of the route.

One possible hypothesis is that bunching is caused by heavy ridership during rush hour (inbound between 7:00 and 10:00 am, and outbound between 4:00 and 6:00 pm). The data does not seem to support this hypothesis. Location seems far more deterministic of bunching than time.

Routes P1 and G2 operate along dedicated busways. Comparing the frequency of bunching for these routes to 61 or 71 routes operating in traffic reveals dramatically less bunching on the busways.

Bunching Timelapse Heatmap

To see this all in motion, click the "play" button below to see a time lapse of the occurrences of bunching.

Data for March 1 - 27, 2016 are combined and presented in a single 18-hour (6:00 am to midnight) time lapse based on the corresponding periods of each day. The heatmap shows spatial frequency of bunching. Since more buses are in operation during rush hour, everything else being equal one would expect to observe more bunching during rush hour (simply by virtue of there being more buses which could bunch).

The timelapse heatmap shows that downtown, Oakland, and the intersection of Murray and Forbes in Squirrel Hill are persistent hot spots for bunching. The movement of bunches through time illustrate that bunching typically snowballs and persists. As above, routes P1 and G2 show comparatively very little bunching.
